### ***Context***
If I were to ask any health professional about the most trending and profitable avenues or specialities right now, their answer would probably be one among Physiotherapy or Dermatology and I was charged with building out both of these verticals during my stint at Visit Health as a product manager.

With the rise in OPD cover in health/life insurance and increasing elective procedures, doctors and surgeons are prescribing physiotherapy as a wellness/recovery/healing process to most of their patients and this made it imperative for Visit health to have Cashless Physiotherapy as an offering to its corporate clients and retail insurers who are dying to offer this as VAS (Value added services) to their stakeholders/customers.

> [!NOTE] Call from our MD
> We started back in Jun'25 with a directive from the MD- we are receiving requests from some of our biggest partners that they want to offer Cashless Physiotherapy to their users so we have to go live with it asap. We already have an offering for 
> reimbursements of physiotherapy sessions. We have an estimate of close to 1-2 CR of ops doors right now, which will only expand once the cashless module is also live. We have to build this live as soon as possible.

This was a decent proof of concept for us to invest resources in the physiotherapy service line. We set up a call with the operations team, the network team, the support team, and the doctor team, with the heads of all of these departments along with the tech, product, and design, to understand what can be the possible journey they have in mind and to scope out the end-to-end flow. This is how the journey began. There were multiple rounds of discussion wherein Webhav's MD explained to us what the client is expecting from us. The team of in-house doctors, led by Dr. Shweta, explained to us how normal, regular physiotherapy booking happens and what should be the flow from a doctor's POV. The network team, who have already been looking at possible partnerships, came in to help us understand what is the rationale in the market, who all providers we can partner up with, and what will be the journey from a partner's perspective, from a physiotherapist's perspective.

On the basis of all of this, including me and my reporting manager Akshir, we delved deep into how the journey should look like, how we can have a combination and sync up between what the physiotherapist would want, what a doctor would want, what a user would want, and what the sponsor, in this case the insurer or the corporate, would want. In the entire discussion, we went back and made a lucid of how the journey would look like, and we floated this across to the leadership team and all the stakeholders. This discussion of what the journey would look like went on for a couple of months, with different versions and iterations being made by me and Akshay. Once the journey was finalized on paper and on the flow, we went to the design team to build up initial wireframes and screens for the design. Using this, I sat down with the designer for every day for close to a week. I shared inspirations; we saw how other players are doing this right now, and found that currently there is no aggregator like us who is offering cashless physiotherapy as a benefit to sponsors or to insurers, so you are kind of a first mover in the market. Although there were a couple of physiotherapists who had a digital play like Qis Pine, who have their app live, we set up discussions with them to understand how they function, how their app functions, and how their UI looks like. On this basis, we created the first iteration of how the user journey would look like.

Now, before we delve deeper into the journey, one thing I would like to point out is that in a model like health tech like ours, it is not a simple consumer app platform but a multi-faceted, layered operation which involves physiotherapists who are our partners whom we have to pay for each service. It involves insurers who are sponsoring the service for their users to whom we are servicing, and then we have to share enough proofs, SOPs, and SLAs with the insurer so that they pay the service fee to us and the coverage to us later on. There is the end user, of course, who is taking these services; there is an operations team who is responsible for managing the product and the order and the journey and the support team, who, in case a user is stuck somewhere, comes in and plays their part to service their problem. Each part of the flow of the function uses different interfaces and different dashboards. While we have a main consumer-facing application, we also have an operations dashboard which the operations team uses. We have a doctor's dashboard which is used for the doctor team to verify and digitize the prescriptions, and we have a partner dashboard called Kia wherein the partner uploads prescriptions, manages their orders, and raises invoices and payouts. Apart from this, there are also internal dashboards for the network team wherein they can map a new network, map their physiotherapy session centers, and everything.

It's a multi-layer, multi-faceted play and service, and each element or each function which comes in the journey has to be serviced and maintained by me as the product manager. Once the consumer app designs are live, we again went back to the same set of people who were involved in the initial discussions to show them the journey and take approvals from there. This process again took a couple of months, with a bunch of different iterations. We had a demo session with the MD later on with the CEO, who, while liking the entire journey, wanted us to make it a bit more salesy, wherein the customer is nudged by the UI, not by the operations team, to take these sessions more frequently to raise more physiotherapy requests and, two, overall increase the utilization of the service. We went back to the drawing board, built the service from scratch, made the designs from scratch, and had an entire design revamp so that the journey is more lucrative, is easier for the customer, with simpler operational flows and an overall better user experience. Once the app design was finalized and closed, we went to the tech team, which includes backend developers, Android developers, iOS developers, and web developers, to help them understand the journey to service all the doubts and then finally to start building out the journey.
