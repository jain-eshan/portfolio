For ten years, "non-technical founder" was a polite way of saying *dependent*.

You had ideas. Someone else had the keys to the kingdom. You waited, negotiated equity, wrote specs that got half-understood, and shipped things that looked nothing like what lived in your head.

That era is over.

## Taste is the new moat

When anyone can generate a React component in thirty seconds, the bottleneck stops being *"who can build it"* and becomes *"who knows what's worth building."* That's a taste problem. A systems-thinking problem. A customer-empathy problem.

Those are exactly the muscles a good PM has been training for years without realising they were training for this moment.

## What I actually do now

I think in flows. I sketch in Figma. I write specs in plain English. Claude turns them into code. I read the code, push back where it feels wrong, and ship.

The work isn't coding. The work is having a clear opinion about *what the thing should feel like* — and being able to hold that opinion steady through every step of implementation.

## The catch

You still have to understand enough to smell when something is off. A URL in a query string. A database without indexes. A component re-rendering on every keystroke. You don't need to write the fix — you need to notice it exists.

That's not technical debt. That's technical literacy. And it's cheap to acquire if you're paying attention.

## What this means

If you've been sitting on an idea waiting for a co-founder, stop. The co-founder is already in your laptop. Your job is to bring the taste.
